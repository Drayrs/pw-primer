The Basics

Magic is a card game played between 2 or more players. At the beginning of each game, players normally begin with 20 life. The goal of the game is to reduce your opponent’s life total to 0.

During the game, players take turns playing cards by spending a resource called mana, which is generated during the game. This is known as casting spells, and is how you win!

Each player owns a deck, called a library, of at least 40 or 60 cards which is shuffled prior to the game and kept face down.

After the libaries are shuffled the players decide who will take the first turn. Each player then draws a hand of 7 cards from their library, and the game begins.

Card Layout

  • Mana Cost: The amount of mana that must be spent to play this card from your hand.
  • Type — Subtype: Every card has a type that determines when it can be played, and what happens to it after you do.
  • Expansion Symbol: The colour of this symbol denotes the card's rarity. Rarer cards tend to be more powerful. The symbol's shape tells you which expansion set the card is from.
  • Text Box: The text in this box describes what happens after the card is played (and sometimes before).
  • Power / Toughness: This text describes what happens after the card is played.

Zones

All cards in a game begin in the players' libraries. Once the players draw their opening hands, and begin taking turns, cards can be placed in several different places, or zones.

After you spend mana to cast a spell (i.e play a card from your hand) that card is placed in a zone depending on its type. If the card is a permanent, it is placed on the battlefield. Otherwise, it is immediately discarded, or placed in the graveyard.

The diagram below shows the different zones in a Magic game. When you play, zones do not need to be laid out as shown; players only need to be able to tell which is which.

  • Library: Every card a player owns begins the game in their library. Anytime a player draws a card, it is drawn from their library. When a player would draw a card, and there are none remaining, that player loses the game.
  • Hand: Cards in your hand can be played by spending mana to pay their mana cost. When you draw cards, they are placed in your hand. Cards can also be returned to your hand by card effects. The maximum number of cards in your hand at the end of your turn is 7. These cards are normally kept hidden from anyone else.
  • Battlefield: Where permanents are placed after being played from your hand. Permanents from your graveyard and library can also be placed on the battlefield due to card effects.
  • Graveyard: When a card is discarded, it is placed into your graveyard.
  • Exile: Due to card effects, cards can be sent to the Exile zone. This is known as exiling. Once exiled, cards can't be interacted with except by specific card effects. Normally, these cards are no longer part of the game.

Card Types

Cards come in a number of types, which dictate when they can be played, where they are placed after being played, and how their rules text is read. Cards are normally played from your hand after paying their mana cost.

When most cards are played they are referred to as spells, and the act of playing a spell is called casting. Land cards are the exception. When a land is played, it does not count as a spell.

After a card is played, one of two things happen. If the card is a permanent it is placed onto the battlefield, where it's rules text contain abilities that affect the game. If it is not a permanent, players follow the card's rules text and then it is discarded.

Each card will have at least 1 type, but some permanents will have multiple types. A common example is Artifact Creature. When a card has multiple types, it means it follows the rules for all its types.

Along with types, cards can also have 1 or more subtypes. A creature might have the elf subtype, as in Creature — Elf, or an enchantment the aura subtype, as in Enchantment — Aura.

Unlike types, subtypes don't affect how you can play cards. Subtypes only provide an additional way for rules text to refer to cards.

Tapping

Permanents on the battlefield can be in one of two states; tapped or untapped. When a card is tapped, it is rotated 90° and cannot be tapped again until it is untapped.

Many rules and card effects will reference whether a card is tapped or not. You can think of it like turning the card on and off, or using it up.

At the beginning of each turn, tapped permanents are untapped.

Mana

Mana is the resource that players spend to play cards from their hands and use their abilities.

Mana comes in 5 different colours, and each has a different mana symbol— red , green , white , blue , and black

There is also colourless mana, with its own mana symbol—

Cards also have colours. If there is a coloured mana symbol in a card's mana cost, the card counts as that colour. If there are no coloured mana symbols, the card is colourless.

The most common, and important, method of generating mana is by tapping Basic Land. There are 5 different basic lands, 1 for each colour in Magic— Mountains, Forests, Plains, Islands, and Swamps. Each basic land produces 1 mana of its type when tapped.

Mana can also be produced by spells and abilities. When mana is generated by a player it is added to their mana pool. Mana in a player's mana pool remains until the end of phases in a turn, or until it is spent.

A mana cost is the amount of mana that must be spent to play cards and activate abilities. The Turn

During a game of Magic each player takes turns. The active player is the player currently taking their turn. Each turn is defined by a set of 5 phases that must be started and finished in order:

  1. Beginning Phase

  2. Main Phase

  3. Combat Phase

  4. Main Phase

  5. Ending Phase

The Beginning, Combat, and Ending phases are broken down further into Steps where specific actions take place. Phases and Steps are what determine when different types of cards can be played, if at all, and are called out by rules to tell the players how long some effects last for. Spells & The Stack

Every card a player plays from their hand, except for Land, is considered a spell. It was stated that what happens afterwords depends on the card's type. This is true, but there is something important that happens in-between.

Spells can only be cast during certain parts of a player's turn. Permanents and Sorceries can only be cast during your Main Phase, and while there is nothing in a special zone called the Stack. However, Instants can be played at almost any time, including on your opponent's turn!

Once a spell is cast, it is placed onto the Stack. After this, each player is given the chance, in turn order, to play Instant spells "in response" (Instants can be played at any time, after all!). Any additional spells are also placed onto the Stack, in the order they were cast.

Once players no longer choose to play spells, the top-most spell on the Stack will resolve. When a permanent resolves, it is placed on the Battlefield. When a sorcery or instant resolves, it has its effect (the instructions written in the card's text box) and is then discarded. Each player is then given another chance to play spells, as before. This continues until there are no longer any spells on the Stack.

Abilities

The text boxes of permanents on the battlefield define abilities which affect the game. Generally, abilities are active only while the permanent is on the battlefield (but not always). There are three types of abilities:

  1. Static Abilities

    These are declarations of things which are true, such as Creatures you control get +1/+1..

  2. Triggered Abilities

    Text which begins with "whenever", "when", and "at" are triggered abilities. When the event described occurs the text happens and can't be delayed; it happens automatically. For instance: When Silvergill Adept enters the battlefield, draw a card..

  3. Activated Abilities

    These abilites are activated by the player. They are easily recognizable by their structure: a cost followed by an effect, and separated by a colon :. For example: : Sun-Collared Raptor gets +3/+0 until end of turn.

    First the cost is paid, and then the ability is activated. This can be done any time a player can play an instant.

    Activated abilities can have many different costs, from discarding cards to sacrificing creatures (place them in the graveyard), and in any combination. The most common of these are tapping the permanent the ability belongs to, denoted by the tap symbol , and mana costs.

    Basic lands each have the ability : Add //// to your mana pool., denoted by the large mana symbol in their text boxes.

Triggered and Activated abilities both go on the Stack. When an ability is triggered or activated it is placed on the Stack just like a spell, and can be responded to in the same way.

Targeting

Some spells and abilities will require you to choose a target. This means that you must choose something for a spell or ability to affect. Valid targets are implied by the spell or ability text. For example: Bombard deals 4 damage to target creature where the player must choose a creature, or Destroy target artifact or enchantment. where the player must choose an artifact or enchantment. If a spell requires a target, or to "choose" an option, the decision must be made when the spell is placed on the Stack.

Unless otherwise stated, the target must be on the Battlefield. A card's text might also specify additional conditions for valid targets, such as target noncreature spell or target creature you control. If a target becomes invalid before the spell or ability resolves, as in it is no longer in the required zone or no longer meets the targeting conditions, it does nothing.

Keywords

Often, Magic cards will use a single word or phrase to summarize abilities and actions, called keywords. New keywords tend to be added with every expansion set, which means there are many, many keywords that can be found on Magic cards. Luckily, there is only a small set that is used frequently!

There are 2 additional keyword abilities and 1 keyword action that are specific to cards with the Aura and Equipment subtypes:

When an Aura is attached to a permanent or player, it is considered to be the enchanted permanent or player. A player chooses a permanent or player for the Aura to enchant when it enters the battlefield.

When an Equipment is attached to a creature it is considered to be the equipped creature. Likewise, a player chooses which creature the Equipment attaches to after the Equip ability is activated. The creature is equipped after the ability resolves.

If an enchanted permanent leaves the battlefield, the Aura is put into its owner’s graveyard. When an equipped creature leaves the battlefield, the equipment is unattached but remains in play. Both events also occur if the equipped or enchanted object is no longer a valid target of Enchant or Equip (no longer the right type for some reason, or because of some other effect).

The Golden Rule

Whenever a card contradicts the rules, the card wins.

For example:

  • You may play more than one land per turn.
  • At the beginning of your draw step, draw an additional card.
  • You have no maximum hand size.

An extreme example of this would be:

You can't lose the game and your opponents can't win the game.
